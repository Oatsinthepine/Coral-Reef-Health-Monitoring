Mapping Coral Reef Health in the Great Barrier Reef

Long-term coral condition, benthic cover, disturbance and protected-area context analysis and visualisation

This interactive narrative visualisation communicates selected findings from the Data Exploration Project (DEP) using long-term chornological Australian Institute of Marine Science (AIMS) reef monitoring data, recent coral health context, and spatial protected-area information. Together, the views below explore where reefs are monitored, how live and dead coral cover has changed over time, how benthic composition shifts, how ecological indicators vary by sector, and how condition and disturbance measures relate to one another.

How to use this visualisation

  • Select a sector to update linked charts.
  • Hover charts to inspect values.
  • Click legends to focus layers.
  • Click heatmap cells to inspect relationships.

Data note

Charts draw on AIMS long-term monitoring records joined with reef coordinates and marine protected-area context. Benthic cover indicators are available for a subset of reef-year records; missing values are excluded from benthic and correlation views. Sector codes follow AIMS management sectors across the Great Barrier Reef.

Live and dead coral cover over time

This chart shows long-term mean live coral and dead coral cover aggregated across monitored reefs. Live coral reflects reef-building capacity; dead coral can signal recent mortality or structural loss. Together they summarise broad condition trends for the selected sector and period.

Hover the lines to read yearly means.

Benthic cover indicators

Hard coral, soft coral, and algae cover describe the benthic structure of monitored reefs. Hard coral represents reef-building habitat; soft coral adds structural diversity; algae can increase when grazing or coral loss shifts competitive balance. The stacked view shows how these three indicators change over time.

Cover values are stacked for visual comparison. They are yearly mean cover indicators and are not constrained to sum to 100%.

Hover over a year to inspect all benthic cover indicators.

Summary and references

Key messages

  • Monitored reefs span AIMS management sectors across the Great Barrier Reef; selecting a sector on the map or in the controls links spatial context with the trend, benthic, and indicator charts.
  • Mean live and dead coral cover change over the 1993–2023 record, with patterns that differ by sector and by the period selected above the charts.
  • Benthic indicators describe complementary aspects of reef structure. The correlation heatmap highlights associations among measures—for example, live coral and hard coral cover tend to move together, while algae cover is generally lower where live coral is higher.
  • Marine protected-area boundaries help situate monitored reefs geographically, but protected status provides context only and does not, on its own, explain the condition patterns shown in the charts.

This dashboard shows that coral reef condition in the Great Barrier Reef is both temporally variable and spatially uneven. Long-term live coral cover fluctuates across the monitoring period, while benthic indicators reveal contrasting patterns between coral cover and algae cover. The sector-level bubble view highlights that ecological indicators do not change uniformly across the reef system. The correlation heatmap provides a compact overview of relationships among reef condition and disturbance indicators, while the map places monitored reef sites within their marine protected-area context. Together, the views support exploratory interpretation rather than causal claims.

Data sources

  • AIMS Long-term Monitoring Program (reef-year indicators and coordinates)
  • MMP Coral 2024 (supplementary coral context)
  • CAPAD Marine 2024 (Collaborative Australian Protected Areas Database — marine areas)

Limitations

  • Benthic cover is available for a subset of reef-year records; charts using these fields exclude incomplete rows.
  • Protected-area status describes spatial context only and does not imply causation of reef condition.
